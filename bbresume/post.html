A diverse reading list is no threat to Shakespeare – every student benefits

As a black student studying English literature, I was rarely reflected in the texts I studied or the people who taught me. A more complete story needs to be told

When I started university, as a black English literature student, I found my programme heavily weighted towards literature written by straight, white, upper-class men. I rarely found myself reflected in the texts I studied or the people who taught me.

In the classroom I suffered the kind of casual ignorance that is a staple part of life for a black girl attempting to move through white academic spaces – from lecturers and students angrily explaining to me why Joseph Conrad’s Heart of Darkness wasn’t racist, to becoming the resident expert on all things non-white the moment issues of race were discussed.

In my experience, influential black writers were mostly relegated to optional courses at the end of the honours degree. A large part of the student body would pass through university without encountering writing by anyone outside of the canon. They might easily leave with an impression that no one outside this Eurocentric, heteropatriarchal list was worthy of study.

These attacks often come from fears that calls for diversity threaten to knock writers such as Shakespeare off the reading list, but this isn’t what we’re asking for. We are campaigning for more nuanced readings of work that acknowledges colonial influence, and for more gender and LGBTQ-inclusive work. The current practice of seeing such works as “other” is wrong. The beneficiaries of a diverse curriculum are not just marginalised people, but everyone.

Project Myopia recently featured an interview with Professor Ngũgĩ Wa Thiong’o, one of the world’s leading postcolonial critics. As he puts it, diversity in education is really an exciting opportunity:

“When you crush hierarchy, and replace it with network, then the cultures held in the different languages generate oxygen. They cross-fertilize. Cultures are able to breathe life into each other. Every culture should be taught with a nod to other cultures… They are all very exciting and it is not necessary to put them in a hierarchical relationship to each other. Let them network.”
